  1st Place Solution | Hill Climbing + Ridge Ensemble

Diabetes Prediction Challenge

<https://www.kaggle.com/competitions/playground-series-s5e12>

Solution Writeup · 1st place · Jan 1, 2026

Happy New Year, Kaggle Community! 🎉

This win came somewhat unexpectedly, but it validates the sustainability
of my favorite Hill Climbing (HC) approach I've been refining over
multiple previous competitions, consistently achieving Top 1% places.
This was an incredible learning experience, and I want to thank everyone
who shared their insights throughout the competition – especially
@masayakawamata <https://www.kaggle.com/masayakawamata> for the shared
post-cutoff cross-validation approach, and @daylighth <https://
www.kaggle.com/daylighth>, @laureanoarcanio <https://www.kaggle.com/
laureanoarcanio>, @tilii <https://www.kaggle.com/tilii>, @mikhailnaumov
<https://www.kaggle.com/mikhailnaumov>, and @siukeitin <https://
www.kaggle.com/siukeitin> for their discussion posts and scripts.

This 1st place finish was particularly satisfying because it came after
a frustrating plateau. My HC ensemble was stuck at post-cutoff CV
0.7088X and Public LB 0.70722. Any addition of new models caused
improvement in CV, but Public LB dropped. I decided to apply Ridge
Ensemble Stacking using the top models from HC selection as input. I
experimented with different alphas on rank-transformed predictions and
different numbers of models. A plateau was achieved here as well, with
post-cutoff CV of 0.70860 and Public LB 0.70739. I stopped at this stage
as the gap between post-cutoff CV and Public LB of 0.00121 seemed
acceptable.

I suppose that the good final result was ensured through leveraging many
diverse base models (tree-based and neural networks) with different
hyperparameters (depth, learning rate, regularization), feature
engineering (ratios, polynomials, target encoding), seeds (multiple runs
for variance reduction). I included in HC both single models and other
ensembles. Such a mixed approach worked for me in previous competitions.

In the Ridge Ensemble, I focused purely on performance (post-cutoff AUC)
rather than correlation-based diversity. The diversity came naturally
from HC's exploration process, which tends to select models that
complement each other.

The Ridge Ensemble (based on Top-36 models from HC and alpha = 10) had
slightly lower post-cutoff CV than HC (0.70860 vs 0.70886), but
performed better on both Public and Private LB. This demonstrates that
stabilization from Ridge regularization sometimes can be more valuable
than raw CV optimization.

With Ridge Ensemble based on Top-34 models and alpha=5, it was possible
to achieve a Private LB of 0.70514, but the Public LB score of this
ensemble was too low (0.70734) to confidently select it for final
submission based on the gap.

Key takeaways in this competition were:
1) distribution shifts are competition killers - if CV improves but LB
drops, it is necessary to investigate data structure;
2) sometimes a slightly lower CV with better generalization wins;
3) two-stage ensembles may improve final results when single-stage
methods reach plateau;
4) test multiple configurations;
5) always trust your CV and trust the process.

Wishing everyone success in upcoming Kaggle competitions, exciting
opportunities in real-world ML projects and continuous learning and
growth in data science.

Hill Climbing is a local search algorithm used in Artificial
Intelligence to find the best solution by continuously moving toward a
better state.
It starts with an initial state and compares it with its neighboring
states. The algorithm moves to the neighbor with a higher value (better
solution). This process continues until no better neighbor is found. At
that point, the algorithm stops.
Hill climbing does not consider all possible paths and does not
backtrack, so it is fast but may get stuck at a local maximum, plateau,
or ridge.

Thank you for your question, @dmitrysavkov <https://www.kaggle.com/
dmitrysavkov>!

I'm happy to share some insights. My main purpose was to create a wide
set of diverse models for Hill Climbing and Ridge Ensemble, rather than
focusing on a single perfect model.

1) Ratio-based features (Interaction Features) capture relationships
between related features. For example, the ratio of "bad" cholesterol to
"good" cholesterol (LDL/HDL) is a standard medical risk indicator:

|def fe_04_ratios(df_train, df_test):
    """Set 04: Ratio features"""
    X, X_test = base_preprocess(df_train, df_test)

    ratio_pairs = [
        ('systolic_bp', 'diastolic_bp'), ('ldl_cholesterol', 'hdl_cholesterol'),
        ('cholesterol_total', 'hdl_cholesterol'), ('triglycerides', 'hdl_cholesterol'),
        ('bmi', 'age'), ('physical_activity_minutes_per_week', 'screen_time_hours_per_day'),
        ('diet_score', 'bmi'), ('sleep_hours_per_day', 'age'),
        ('heart_rate', 'age'), ('cholesterol_total', 'bmi'),
        ('triglycerides', 'cholesterol_total'), ('ldl_cholesterol', 'cholesterol_total')
    ]

    for c1, c2 in ratio_pairs:
        if c1 in X.columns and c2 in X.columns:
            X[f'{c1}_div_{c2}'] = X[c1] / (X[c2] + 1e-8)  # +1e-8 prevents division by zero
            X_test[f'{c1}_div_{c2}'] = X_test[c1] / (X_test[c2] + 1e-8)

    return X, X_test

|
content_copy

The |+ 1e-8| (0.00000001) is added here to prevent division by zero errors.

2) Polynomial features capture non-linear relationships by creating
squared and cubed versions of numeric features:

|# Use top 10 numeric columns to avoid feature explosion
for col in NUMERIC_COLS[:10]:
    if col in X.columns:
        # Square features (captures U-shaped relationships)
        X[f'{col}_sq'] = X[col] ** 2
        X_test[f'{col}_sq'] = X_test[col] ** 2

        # Cube features (captures S-shaped relationships)
        X[f'{col}_cb'] = X[col] ** 3
        X_test[f'{col}_cb'] = X_test[col] ** 3

print(f"After polynomial features - Train shape: {X.shape}")
print(f"After polynomial features - Test shape: {X_test.shape}")

|
content_copy

Here polynomial features are used only on the most important features
(top 10-15), otherwise they will become too many and will slow down
training.

3) Original dataset aggregation - yes, in the majority of my models I
concatenated the original diabetes dataset with the competition training
data to increase sample size and improve models' stability. The combined
data was used only for training, with all preprocessing and encoding
performed inside cross-validation folds.

4) For target encoding I recommend checking out Kaggle notebooks of
@masayakawamata <https://www.kaggle.com/masayakawamata> and @ravi20076
<https://www.kaggle.com/ravi20076> that implement and explain this
concept clearly.

Thank you so much, @tilii7 <https://www.kaggle.com/tilii7> ! I really
appreciate your kind words and congratulations.

I experimented today with different alpha configurations across my Ridge
ensemble of 34 models and was able to push Private LB score even to
0.70514 through systematic manual alpha tuning. Automated RidgeCV
optimizing on CV in this competition would pick a0.1 (highest CV
0.70875) which gives worse private score (0.70504 vs 0.70514) and the
optimal alphas (a6-a8 range) would be missed.

I think what's particularly exciting is that both your win in S5E10
Predicting Road Accident Risk competition and my win here demonstrate
the remarkable potential of Hill Climbing approach.

In the S5E9 Predicting the Beats-per-Minute of Songs competition the
winner made an incredible 721-position jump from public to private LB.
Unfortunately, no solutions were published there, and the dataset had
very weak signal, so it's hard to draw firm conclusions. I was on 18th
place there (Top 1%) with Hill Climbing solution and that experience
reinforced my confidence in continuing to explore and refine this
approach by combing it with Ridge.
