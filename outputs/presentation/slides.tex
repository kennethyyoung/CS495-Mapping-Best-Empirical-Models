\section{Introduction}
% =================================================================


% ---------------- What is Kaggle ----------------
\begin{frame}{What is Kaggle?}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Largest public platform for machine-learning \alert{competitions}
  \item People submit predictive models against a shared dataset
  \item Submissions are ranked by a stated score -- accuracy, RMSE, AUC
  \item Two competition types matter here:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Featured} -- sponsor-funded, real prize money
    \item \textbf{Playground Series} -- monthly, no prize, synthetic data
  \end{itemize}
  \item After each contest ends, winners post a public \alert{writeup}
  \item Writeups are the raw material for this study
\end{itemize}

\note{
Kaggle is the largest public platform for machine-learning competitions.
The format is simple: a sponsor or Kaggle itself publishes a dataset,
sets a scoring metric, and competitors spend a month or two trying to
build the highest-scoring model.

Two types of competitions are in scope for this study. Featured
competitions have real prize money, typically tens of thousands of
dollars. Playground Series competitions are monthly contests Kaggle
runs themselves with synthetic data -- mostly as a learning venue.

After the competition ends, the winners post a writeup that describes
what they did. That writeup is the raw material for this study.
}
\end{frame}


% ---------------- Research arc (merged + pivot framing) ----------------
\begin{frame}{The research arc}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Original question:} which feature engineering techniques do winners use?
  \begin{itemize}
    \item ``Ensembles of tree models win'' is already common knowledge
    \item Which specific FE techniques is \emph{not} well documented
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{What we hit in the data:} the FE question couldn't be answered cleanly
  \item \textbf{Re-framed question:}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item What \alert{kind of strategy} wins?
    \item And how does each kind connect to the \alert{data conditions} of the competition?
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\note{
The research arc has two stages.

The original question was: which feature engineering techniques do
winners actually use? This is a substantive question because the
high-level answer is already common knowledge -- ensembles of
tree-based models dominate tabular competitions. But the specific
feature-engineering choices that separate winners from runners-up are
not well documented in the practitioner literature.

We tried to answer that question. We hit a wall, which I'll show you
in two slides. So we re-framed: instead of asking which FE techniques
win, we asked what kind of strategy wins, and how does that connect
to the data conditions of the competition. That re-framing is what
produces the typology you'll see in the results.
}
\end{frame}

\section{Prior Studies}
% =================================================================


% ---------------- Related work + gap (simplified) ----------------
\begin{frame}{What's been done -- and what hasn't}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Academic ML research:} compares \emph{algorithms} on benchmark datasets
  \begin{itemize}
    \item ``Which model is best on dataset X?''
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Practitioner blog posts:} describe individual winning solutions
  \begin{itemize}
    \item ``Here's how I won this competition.''
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Neither systematically characterizes what strategies recur across many wins}
\end{itemize}
\vspace{8pt}
\alert{The gap:} Treat the Kaggle community as a \textbf{population to study}, not just a \textbf{source of techniques}.

\note{
Two existing strands of literature touch this space.

First, academic machine-learning research compares one algorithm to
another on standardized benchmark datasets. Studies will tell you that
XGBoost beats logistic regression on tabular data, or that a particular
neural-network architecture is competitive with gradient-boosted trees.
But these studies compare algorithms on fixed datasets -- they don't
tell you what winning solutions across many competitions actually do.

Second, practitioner blog posts and tutorial notebooks describe
individual winning strategies. Someone wins a competition, they write
up what they did, and that becomes folk knowledge. But each post is
one case study; there's no systematic look across many wins.

The gap is straightforward: nobody's empirically characterized
winning Kaggle strategies as a population. That's what this study does.
}
\end{frame}

% ---------------- Pivot slide (NEW) ----------------
\begin{frame}{From flowchart to typology: a pivot}
\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
\column{0.44\textwidth}
\raggedright
\small
\textbf{Original goal:}\\
derive a feature-engineering decision flowchart

\vspace{6pt}
\textbf{What the data showed:}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item GBM dominated \alert{78\%} -- no real ``which model'' branch
  \item FE was free text: \alert{166 unique tags, 163 appear only once}
  \item Several fields below the 70\% completeness threshold
\end{itemize}

\vspace{4pt}
\textbf{Pivot:} characterize by \alert{paradigm}, not by individual FE technique

\column{0.53\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{phase5_62_model_family.png}\\[2pt]
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{phase5_61_fe_tag_frequency.png}
\end{columns}

\note{
This is the most important methodology slide. Let me be candid about
the pivot.

The original goal was to build a decision flowchart from the data.
Feed in the dataset's properties and have the flowchart recommend
which feature-engineering steps to take.

The two charts on the right show what the data actually looked like.
Top right: gradient-boosted machines were the dominant model family
in 78 percent of wins. The other model families are tiny by
comparison. So there was no meaningful ``which model'' branch in
the flowchart.

Bottom right: feature engineering was recorded as free-text prose
in the writeups. When I tried to bucket the tags, I got 166 unique
tags across 45 wins, and 163 of those -- 98 percent -- appear
exactly once. You can't build a frequency chart from that. Each
winner's feature engineering is essentially custom.

A few fields that were supposed to be decision-node inputs --
scaling, outlier treatment, distribution shift -- were filled in
less than 70 percent of the time.

So we asked the honest question: is feature engineering really
what distinguishes winners, or is something else? The pivot was to
characterize winners by paradigm -- the overall kind of strategy
they used -- instead of trying to bucket their individual FE
techniques. Several of the ``contradicted'' patterns you'll see
in the results are direct evidence that the original FE-flowchart
approach wouldn't have generalized.
}
\end{frame}


% ---------------- Two-pass coding (modified to emphasize WHY) ----------------
\begin{frame}{Two-pass coding design}
\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
\column{0.48\textwidth}
\raggedright
\textbf{Pass 1: structured schema}
\begin{itemize}
  \item 38-column spreadsheet
  \item Models, ensembling, CV, encoding, FE
  \item Captures \alert{what was used}
\end{itemize}

\column{0.48\textwidth}
\raggedright
\textbf{Pass 2: qualitative re-evaluation}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Per-writeup, nine-section template
  \item Origination vs.\ inheritance
  \item Paradigm classification
  \item Captures \alert{what was originated}
\end{itemize}
\end{columns}

\vspace{12pt}
\centering
\small
\alert{Pass 1 can tell you that target encoding was used. It cannot tell you whether the winner originated the technique or copied it from a public notebook.}

\note{
The methodological centerpiece. Pass 1 is the structured spreadsheet
I just described -- 38 columns capturing what techniques each winner
used. That's good for distributional analysis: how often does XGBoost
appear, what's the modal CV strategy, that kind of thing.

But Pass 1 has a critical blind spot. It can tell me that target
encoding was used in a winning solution. It can't tell me whether
the winner originated that technique or whether they inherited it
from a publicly-shared notebook by another community member.

That distinction matters a lot for the typology and especially for
the community-attribution analysis you'll see later. So I did a
second pass -- re-reading each writeup with a nine-section template
that explicitly separates origination from inheritance. The two-pass
design is itself a methodological contribution, and it's replicable.
}
\end{frame}


% ---------------- Audit + corrections (simpler language) ----------------
\begin{frame}{Double-checking our own work}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Pass 2 surfaced cell-level errors in Pass 1
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Example: one entry had \texttt{n\_rows = 300{,}000}, writeup said \alert{4 million} (two CSV files were loaded together)
  \end{itemize}
  \item Built a dedicated \texttt{Data Quality Audit} sheet
  \begin{itemize}
    \item 24 issues catalogued, color-coded by severity
  \end{itemize}
  \item Applied corrections in severity order (high $\to$ medium $\to$ low)
  \item Recorded every change in a separate \texttt{Corrections Log} sheet
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Old value, new value, reason, source
  \end{itemize}
  \item Added two columns the audit identified as missing (\texttt{top\_3\_margin}, \texttt{distribution\_shift\_type})
\end{itemize}

\note{
When I re-read the writeups for Pass 2, I started finding cell-level
errors in Pass 1. For example, one entry had n-rows coded as 300,000
when the writeup explicitly said 4 million training rows were loaded.
I had counted one CSV file but missed a second one that the winner
loaded alongside it. Small thing, but it changed what kind of
problem that entry was -- 4 million rows supports a very different
strategy than 300,000.

I built a dedicated audit sheet to catalog every issue I found,
color-coded by severity. Then I applied corrections in severity
order -- high first, then medium, then low -- and recorded every
change in a separate corrections log with the old value, the new
value, the reason, and the source.

I also added two columns the audit identified as missing. That
audit trail is what makes the work replicable and defensible.
}
\end{frame}


% ---------------- Limitations ----------------
\begin{frame}{Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Small sample.} N = 45 limits sub-group analysis
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Ensemble-stacking group (n = 28) is reliable
    \item Smaller paradigms (n = 3--6) are descriptive only
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Single coder.} No formal inter-rater check
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Pass-2 audit on Pass-1 codings caught 17 cell-level errors
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Writeup availability.} Excludes wins where no writeup was published
  \item \textbf{Winners only.} No runner-up or non-top-three comparison
  \item \textbf{Detection limit.} 200-character ``unique edge'' field may miss subtle signals
\end{itemize}

\note{
Limitations to be transparent about.

Sample size of 45 is small for sub-group analysis. The dominant
paradigm has 28 entries which supports cross-tabulation reasonably
well, but the smaller paradigms only have three to six entries each,
so claims about them are descriptive only.

I'm the only coder. There's no formal inter-rater reliability check.
The Pass 2 audit on Pass 1 codings serves as a partial substitute --
it caught 17 cell-level errors, which is a structured second-pass
check -- but it's not the same as a second independent coder.

I only see solutions with published writeups, which is a real
selection effect.

I only have winners, not losers. So I can describe what winners do,
but I can't directly tell you what separates winners from competitors.

And the coupling-evidence framework I'll show next uses a
200-character summary field as its strategy detector. For couplings
whose signal is subtle, the verdict may be partly detection-limited
rather than actually contradicted.
}
\end{frame}

\note{
The first headline. The 45 winning solutions reduce to four paradigms
covering 96 percent of the corpus.

The obvious finding -- ensemble-stacking is the majority, 62 percent --
is the one most people in this room already expect. The more
interesting finding is the other 38 percent. The wins that don't do
the ensemble-stacking thing don't randomly do other things; they
split into three specialist strategies, each of which requires
specific data conditions.

Single-model heavy-FE works at extreme data sizes. Lookup-exploit
needs an exploitable generator artifact and clusters at small data.
Problem-fit neural networks only appear when no external original
dataset is available. The typology is conditional -- not a universal
recipe.

And one finding I didn't expect when I started: there are zero pure
forks in this corpus. Every single winner contributed at least one
meaningful original element. The category of ``win-by-pure-copy''
is empty.
}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{How winners actually build solutions}
\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
\column{0.44\textwidth}
\raggedright
\small
\begin{itemize}\itemsep3pt
  \item \textbf{67\%} draw heavily on community work

\end{itemize}

\vspace{6pt}
\alert{Two myths, both wrong:}

\textit{``Winners just copy popular notebooks''} -- no, 0\% do this

\textit{``Winners build everything from scratch''} -- no, 67\% inherit heavily

\column{0.53\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{phase5_63_origination_score.png}
\end{columns}

\note{
This answers a question newcomers often have: what does winning Kaggle
actually look like as a process?

Two contradictory myths circulate in the community. The cynical view
says people just copy popular public notebooks and submit them. My
data contradicts that -- zero winners do this. Every single one of
the 45 winners contributes at least one meaningful original element.

The heroic view says real winners build everything from scratch. My
data also contradicts that -- two thirds of winners draw heavily on
community work in some form. Eighteen percent explicitly start from a
single forked public notebook. Seven percent are pure
community-template-tweak wins where the public notebook is the
engine and the winner adds one meaningful tweak.

The honest picture is in the middle. Heavy inheritance from community
work plus one meaningful original contribution. That's what winning
actually looks like.
}
\end{frame}


% ---------------- Coupling evidence (KEEP) ----------------
\begin{frame}{Coupling evidence: only 2 of 6 promoted patterns survive}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{phase5_51_coupling_evidence.png}
\\[6pt]
{\color{kc-muted}\scriptsize Source: \texttt{phase5\_51\_coupling\_evidence.png}}

\note{
This is the analytical centerpiece. Pass 2 identified six promoted
constraint-to-strategy couplings -- patterns that appeared in multiple
writeups and looked like real community practice. To test each one
quantitatively, I defined a constraint filter and a strategy filter,
then counted supporting, contradicting, and neutral cases.

Two couplings survive at the strong-evidence threshold.

Linear-stacker dominance: 32 of 40 testable cases. When winners
ensemble, they overwhelmingly use a linear stacker -- Ridge regression,
mean blending, hill climbing -- rather than a nonlinear stacker like
a neural network or another tree model.

Distribution-shift-driven custom CV: four of five testable cases.
Small sample, treat with caution.

Four couplings are contradicted. The most striking is the next slide.
}
\end{frame}


% ---------------- Use as columns correction (NEW) ----------------
\begin{frame}{The ``use as columns'' correction}
\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
\column{0.42\textwidth}
\raggedright
\small
\textbf{Widely-shared Kaggle tip:}

\textit{``When the original public dataset is available, attach it as new \textbf{columns} to your training data.''}

\vspace{6pt}
\alert{The community pattern is rows-only concatenation.}

\vspace{4pt}
The ``as columns'' advice reflects \textbf{one author's} idiosyncratic practice.

\column{0.55\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{phase5_64_use_mode_breakdown.png}
\end{columns}

\note{
This is the single most actionable finding -- a direct contradiction
of a widely-cited Kaggle technique.

There's a famous piece of Kaggle advice attributed to one prominent
author: when the original public dataset is available for a synthetic
competition, attach it as new columns to your training data, not just
as more rows.

My data says this is wrong for almost everyone. Of the 30 winners
in my corpus with an available original dataset, seventeen just added
it as more training rows. The boring concatenation. Only one used
columns-only. Two chose not to use it at all because their cross-
validation said it would hurt.

The ``as columns'' trick reflects one author's practice. The community
pattern is plain rows-only. A practitioner who reads that famous
writeup and assumes everyone does this would be working from a
misleading template.

This is the kind of finding the work contributes -- not new techniques,
but corrections to community folk knowledge.
}
\end{frame}

First: pure technique-source. The most-cited handle in the entire

siukeitin has never appeared as a top-three winner in any of my 45

for the top three.

Second: member who occasionally wins. tilii7 has one win plus eight
citations. masayakawamata has one win plus five citations. They
win sometimes, and they're widely cited as sources by other winners.

Third: dominant competitor also cited. cdeotte has four wins plus
six citations. Same for mahog.

The note at the bottom is the cdeotte timeline finding. His earliest
documented presence in the corpus is February 2023, commenting on
someone else's winning writeup as an outside observer. His first
win in the corpus is December 2024 -- 22 months later. The
observe-before-win arc is concrete in his case.

This empirical separation -- pure source, occasional winner,
dominant cited competitor -- is the strongest example I have of
the originator-versus-canonizer gap in competitive machine learning.
The most-cited technique source in my corpus has not won a top-three
position.
}

% ---------------- Closer: what this work contributes (NEW) ----------------
\begin{frame}{What this work contributes}
\textbf{(beyond ``ensembles win'')}
\vspace{8pt}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A typology with constraint structure}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Four paradigms cover 96\% of wins

  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A correction to community folk knowledge}

\note{
To close: three things this work contributes beyond what an audience
member already knows.

First, a typology with constraint structure. The four paradigms cover
96 percent of wins, and three of the four align with measurable data
conditions. So it's not just a labeling exercise -- it tells you

Second, a correction to community folk knowledge. Four of six
